Momentum Gains and Competitive Buy-Side Trading Strategies
A python Backtrader implementation of the algorithmic trading strategy described in the following paper.
Original paper
üìï¬†Buy-Side Competition and Momentum Profits.pdf
Trading rules
Obtain data on actively managed, open-ended U.S. equity mutual funds (e.g., from CRSP Survivor-Bias Free US Mutual Fund database)
Exclude financial firms and firms with a price below $1 at the end of the month
Compute the momentum competition metric:
At the end of month t-1, calculate returns over the interval (t-12, t-2) and standardize each stock‚Äôs return to a z-score
Locate each mutual fund in the one-dimensional momentum space based on the value-weighted location of the stocks it holds
Define two funds as competitors at time t if the interfund distance is less than or equal to a chosen parameter for target granularity
Compute the competition metric based on the similarity between a fund and its spatially proximate rivals
Average the competition measure across all funds holding a particular stock
At the end of each month t-1:
Sort stocks into terciles by momentum competition
Sort stocks within the lowest competition tercile into quintile portfolios based on their traditional 12-month momentum
Long the highest quintile and short the lowest quintile
Employ a value-weighted strategy and rebalance monthly
Why it works
The theory suggests that buy-side investors exploit profit opportunities in financial markets through appropriate trading strategies. Buy-side price pressure is stronger when more investors are present in a profitable market, leading to the definition of a measure for buy-side competition for momentum profits. The paper hypothesizes that momentum generates larger profits when buy-side competition is low, as investing rents are limited when investors compete more vigorously. The strategy focuses on large capitalization stocks, where mutual funds are likely to be the marginal investor. The hypothesis is supported by the finding that the momentum spread is 139 basis points when buy-side competition is low but insignificant when competition is high.
Hedging stocks in bear markets?
This trading strategy‚Äôs correlation with equity market risk is unknown, as the source and related research papers do not provide insight on this aspect. The strategy is designed as a long-short, but can be divided into two parts: the long leg, which is likely strongly correlated to the equity market, and the short-only leg, which may serve as a hedge during market downturns. However, a rigorous backtest is required to determine the strategy‚Äôs return/risk characteristics and correlation to the market.
