Modeling
The goal of modeling is to capture common joint phenomena. You will differentiate between regressive models and joint distribution models for practical reasons, connecting the results afterwards to validate the consistency of the obtained results.
All parametric modeling should be preceded by a non-parametric analysis to guide the choice of models. You will use systematic methods to measure the overfitting of estimated parameters, and address eigen-dimensional estimates systematically.
Explorations
Non-parametric explorations
Non-parametric methods are useful for exploration but are generally less efficient for prediction or anticipation due to overlearning. These methods help you understand the relationships between variables before choosing a model. You will typically use informational methods, but the type of relationship may not be capturable by any model.
These analyses can help you discard certain characteristics, reducing dimensionality.
Conjunctions of rare events
Once the number of potentially explanatory features is reduced, you will try to measure whether there are rare event conjunctions for Y and X. You will look for Bayesian thresholds that maximize the probability of rare events between X and Y.
You will explore simple Markov models, keeping in mind that more sophisticated conditioning results in fewer elements in each "state" of the Markov chain, impacting the reliability of the obtained results.
Searching for rare event conjunctions will be part of the systematic tests to set up.
Regression vs. joint distribution
After the previous phase, you will have isolated a series of potentially explanatory variables X(1), ..., X(N) relative to a variable to be explained Y. You will then need to set up a joint behavior model between Y and X, moving from the least complex models to the most complex ones.
If you believe there is an underlying linear relationship between X's and Y's, you need a regression model
If you think that what you observe on (X, Y) is a typical realization of their joint locus, you need to use a joint law model
This is easily illustrated in the case of a two dimensional cloud.
There is no absolute good or bad model, and that's why you must understand the mechanisms updated by your modeling and use non-parametric models as little as possible for production.
Regression models
Regression models are formalized by:
Y(t+1) = F(X(1,t),..., X(N,t)) + noise
Iterative regression methods have some shortcomings but will be used in the absence of better alternatives.
Linear regressions minimize an L2 distance and are sensitive to extreme values. The descriptive analysis of variables can help you use log transformations or scores. However, keep in mind that log transformations can be misleading, and scores can be projected in a Gaussian universe when the regression methods work better on Gaussian clouds.
Joint distribution models
Joint distribution models are formalized by:
(Y(t), X(1,t), ..., X(N,t))~ g(Y, X(1), ..., X(N))
Regression and joint law models will be distinguished by their usage:
Regression models are used to predict or anticipate
Joint law models are used to predict or value a realization
Gaussian mixtures are the most common joint distribution models and will be used in a standard way. Bayesian networks are more generic but require larger datasets and are prone to overlearning.
In general, you can "exchange data for knowledge" in modeling. The more you identify explicit behavior, the less you will need long histories and be exposed to overlearning risks.
