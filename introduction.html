Introduction
Quantitative trading involves programming computers to execute trades. It is one of the most lucrative jobs for recent college graduates, with a base salary typically starting at $100,000, plus potentially significant bonuses. The work offers flexible hours, no strict dress code, and uses cutting-edge technology. The job is both fast-paced and intellectually stimulating, with performance evaluated objectively.
The field of quant trading merges programming and trading, resulting in a highly efficient process for turning ideas into cash. Compared to writing a book or constructing a building, programming is the fastest way to translate a concept into a tangible outcome. On the other hand, trading is a direct path to earning money, unlike launching a startup or building a retail chain, which can require significant upfront investment and time to yield returns. By combining programming and trading, quant trading provides the most efficient route from an idea to profit.

The different facets of the job
Developing ML strategies on your own can be a challenging task, as it requires significant effort and expertise in several areas such as data curation and processing, software development, feature analysis, and backtesting. Additionally, high-performance computing infrastructure and execution simulators are needed, making the process complex.
The organization of the process for creating trading strategies
The process of discovering investment strategies within modern asset managers is divided into different stations or teams, each with specific responsibilities.
The first station is the Data Curators, responsible for collecting, cleaning, indexing, storing, adjusting, and delivering data to the production chain.
The second station is the Feature Analysts, responsible for transforming raw data into informative signals with some predictive power over financial variables.
The third station is the Strategists, responsible for developing investment algorithms from informative features and formulating a general theory that explains them.
The fourth station is the Backtesters, responsible for assessing the profitability of an investment strategy under various scenarios.
The fifth station is the Deployment Team, responsible for integrating the strategy code into the production line.
Finally, there is the Portfolio Oversight station, which follows the lifecycle of the strategy from embargo to decommission.
The elements that make up a strategy
Data:
Problem: Low-quality data leads to low-quality results.
Solution: Use unique, difficult-to-manipulate data. If you're the only one with access to the data, it gives you a competitive edge.
Software
Problem: Specialized tasks require customized tools.
Solution: Create your own classes and avoid relying on popular libraries that everyone else is using.
Hardware
Problem: Machine learning involves computationally intensive tasks.
Solution: Become an expert in high-performance computing (HPC) or partner with a National Laboratory to build a supercomputer.
Math
Problem: Proving mathematical theorems can take years or even centuries.
Solution: Use experimental math to solve hard, intractable problems. For example, Bailey, Borwein, and Plouffe discovered a spigot algorithm for pi without proof.
Meta-Strategies
Problem: Amateurs develop individual strategies, while professionals develop methods to mass-produce strategies.
Solution: Approach your research lab like a factory and prioritize methodical hard work over inspiration. This was the philosophy of physicist Ernest Lawrence, founder of the first U.S. National Laboratory.
Overfitting
Problem: Cross-validation methods often fail in finance, leading to false discoveries.
Solution:
Be skeptical of your own work and constantly challenge yourself to prove your value.
Overfitting is unethical and leads to unfulfilled promises. It's a waste of time and resources and can harm your reputation.
Focus on out-of-sample returns to create substantial wealth for your investors.
