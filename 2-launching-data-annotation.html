1. Define the project
1. 1. Identify key stakeholders
Defining the project involves identifying the key stakeholders, establishing project goals, and having communication tools in your team. There are three key stakeholders in a data labeling project:
The project manager who determines the practical application of the project and understands its potential business impact.
The annotation project manager is responsible for day-to-day oversight of the project, ensuring data quality, consistent labeling, and may have subject matter expertise.
Finally, the engineering manager is primarily responsible for implementing the solution, indicating the models.

1.2. Plan timelines
It is crucial that all stakeholders are involved in constructing the timelines, which should include data preparation, annotation, implementation of the solution, and product development if the model is not the end solution.
There may be dependencies between milestones, such as needing a certain amount of annotated data before starting training. It's essential to keep this in mind when constructing timelines.
Milestones should be time-boxed and based on the complexity of the project and the amount of data to be labeled. The complexity of the milestone and the amount of data to be labeled can impact the duration of the milestone, especially if the annotations are more complex.

1.3. Build the team
After creating your timelines and defining the project goals, the next step is to gather your team. You will need several people to create your training data:
Labelers or annotators who will be in the front line of creating annotations on images or documents
Machine learning models or simple rules can be used to preprocess the assets and for instance prioritize examples
Reviewers are also essential to have above the labelers to validate labels and ensure consistency.
The ML engineer, who is the end user of the training data, should also be involved in the labeling process to ensure the created labels are useful for the model.
It's crucial to have enough labelers on your team.

1.4. Select a data annotation tool
Now that you have your team, the next step is to select a labeling tool. There are three important dimensions to consider when choosing the right platform for your project.
Firstly, make sure that the platform is designed to be productive and label and annotate fast.
Secondly, it should be designed to take high-quality labels and be able to explore, find, and fix issues identified by labelers or annotators while editing.
Lastly, ensure that the platform can be integrated into your existing stack of tools for data storage and frameworks used in a data-centric AI approach. It's crucial to have a platform that can be integrated deeply to help with the labeling process.
With your team and platform in place, your project is ready to start.
1.5. Set project requirements
To create your labeling project on the platform, start by uploading your assets, such as images, to the tool. You can create a basic project by selecting the type of assets you will add, the task you need to achieve, and add your assets from your local computer or remote storage.
Once your assets are on the tool, you can create your ontology or the different categories of labels you want to annotate. You can create a more complex ontology, including classification, object detection jobs, bounding boxes, polygon post estimations, and transcription jobs.

1.6. Create annotation guidelines
To ensure the quality of your labeling project, use your quality expectations to create clear and detailed instructions for your labelers. These instructions should be guidelines or documents that you share with your labelers, providing examples of how to label objects, entities, and other categories. Include edge cases, borderline cases, and confusing examples to ensure clarity for your labelers.
You can find helpful tips on how to build a guideline here.
1.7. Train workforce
To ensure your labelers understand the task and label correctly, you need to train them first.
Start by using a test or training project, where you can verify their work and ensure the level is good enough before moving to the actual project. You can use dedicated metrics such as the honeypot level, where you ask your labelers to label the same images or documents and compare their results to your own.
Use review in the production project. Consider doing at least 10% review to ensure quality. Once you have more data, you can adjust your approach accordingly.
1.8. Set task distribution
Alright, so now that you have your team trained, it's time to assign them to the labeling task. Keep in mind that there may be some subjectivity involved in the labeling process due to individual experience, language skills, and interpretation. When assigning data to your labelers, you have the power to prioritize and assign specific specifications.
For example, in a use case where we're automating the processing of accident statements, we can filter the documents by language and assign them to labelers who are fluent in that language, such as assigning French documents to Paul who is a good French speaker.
You can also prioritize certain documents by assigning them a higher priority level so that your labeling effort can be focused on them first. This can be done using both the UI and the API, and you can also use models such as language detection or Active Learning to help you properly prioritize your data for labeling.
2. Label
2.1. Automated vs manual labeling
We're ready to start labeling now, and while it may seem complex, it's mostly a manual process. However, to be efficient, you can make use of technology to help you. One way is to take advantage of existing business rules, like using lists of products in scripts for a preliminary label. This is known as rule-based automation, which can include dictionaries or regular expressions.
The next step is manual labeling, where you'll mostly be labeling by hand, but you can also make use of interactive and smart tools like tracking or interactive segmentation. For example, you can track an object on a first frame and it will be automatically extrapolated on your description.
Lastly, when you have enough data, you can consider machine learning to extract data and train a machine model. With Kili's API, you can pre-annotate your data. However, it's crucial to not start machine learning too early in the project, as it could bias or confuse the labelers. We suggest doing this only when you're in the last 20% of the project.
Remember, the key to shifting your data set from good to great is to leverage pre-labeling, but be mindful of when you choose to start the process.

Pro tips
Use business rules that already exist to create preliminary labels and leverage rule-based automation to speed up the process.
Use interactive tools like tracking or interactive segmentation to assist with manual labeling.
Consider using machine learning to pre-annotate your data, but wait until you have enough data and avoid starting too early in the project to avoid bias or confusion for labelers.
2.2. Quality management
Now that you have labels and annotations from your labelers, it's important to ensure their quality. This is where your quality management workflow comes in. There are three lines of defense to get high-quality data.
The first line is reviewing. You can set up a workflow where super validators have a look at a portion of the label data and create issues to mark annotations as being wrong and send them back to the labelers. This is the first line of defense.
The second line is using metrics like the Consensus or the Human Model IU to target the right assets for review. When you have these metrics, you can be more specific and review only the assets that matter.
The third line of defense is automation. You can use rule-based approaches to check for errors. For example, you can check if the labelers have included the correct number of vertices in a human body.
You can leverage the interface that you have as a reviewer in the key interface to compare labels manually and use filters to focus your review effort on the most valuable elements to review. You can filter by labeler, labeling class, defects, consensus level, or human model IU metric.
By following these steps, you can ensure that your labeling process is efficient and produces high-quality data.

2.3. Iterate
Now that you have set up the labeling process, it's time to start iterating. However, it's important to keep in mind the last two steps of the process and why they are important. The goal is to start small and gradually increase the workload to ensure the highest quality of labeled data.
Labeling can be a difficult and challenging task. At the beginning, your labelers may make mistakes and your instructions may not be optimal. There may be some edge cases that aren't accounted for in the instructions. Therefore, you should start small and only increase the workload once you've verified the quality of the labeled data.
To do this, we suggest defining sprints that last two or three days, during which you label some data and compute quality metrics. After each sprint, you update your instructions, your workforce, and your workflow. For example, you may update the quality coverage of your review or the confidence of your consensus parameters. Then, you start again with a new batch of data to be labeled.
The first batch should be small because at the beginning, you're not sure how well your labeling process will work. Typically, the first iteration is done by the machine learning engineer himself, labeling 50 to 100 examples by hand. This allows them to understand the data and the labeling task better. They can also evaluate if their ontology is complete and if the tools they're offering to their labelers are optimal and easy to use.
After this first iteration, you should have more complete instructions and a better understanding of the labeling process. You can then ask some labelers in your organization to start labeling, maybe 100 to 200 examples at a time. You should verify that your instructions are clear enough and understood properly by reviewing 100 labeled examples. Once you're comfortable with the quality of the labeled data, you can increase the workload by adding more data or more labelers to label up to 500 data points.
Keep iterating until you have enough labeled data and the quality is consistent. You'll know that you have enough labeled data when your model's performance is good on your test set. At this point, you can export your model to be used by your clients or customers. Remember, it's important to start small, iterate, and gradually increase the workload to ensure the highest quality of labeled data.
