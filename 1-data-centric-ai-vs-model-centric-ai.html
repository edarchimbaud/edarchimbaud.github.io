1. Traditional machine learning is model centric
Traditional machine learning is a model-centric approach where the primary focus is on learning different types of models and architectures, tuning hyper-parameters, loss functions, and regularizations to avoid overfitting. In an ML class, for example, the goal is to train a model that accurately predicts whether a new image is a cat or a dog. However, reality is not as simple as the datasets used in school. Real-world data sets are often not well-curated and can have all sorts of anomalies. Therefore, it makes more sense to focus on fixing issues with the data rather than using fancy ML tricks.
It may come as a surprise to some, but ten of the most commonly cited and used test sets in the field of machine learning have wrong labels. For instance, scorpions are labeled as ticks, and cakes are labeled as rakes. These examples emphasize the importance of exploring and fixing data errors rather than tinkering with models to avoid the garbage in, garbage out issue.
In our Data-Centric AI course, we'll discuss two forms of data-centric AI.
The first form is AI algorithms that understand something about data and use this information to help a model train better. For example, in curriculum learning, an algorithm identifies which data is probably easy to learn, such as starting with simple addition problems in the classroom.
The second form is modifying the data set to directly improve the performance of a model. Confident learning, for instance, removes label errors in a data set before training.
To illustrate the relevance of data-centric AI, we'll look at a text classification task in the lab for today's lecture. The task has some bad data mixed in, and we'll start with model-centric approaches to classify the data. However, the data is not great, and we'll have to figure out how to improve it to get a better classifier.

3. Model centric AI vs data-centric AI
In today's lecture, we will be discussing the difference between Model Centric AI and Data Centric AI.
Model Centric AI is the traditional way of thinking about AI, where the goal is to produce the best model given a dataset. Classically, you change the model to improve its performance on an AI task. However, this approach has limitations. It assumes that the model is the most important aspect of the AI process, and that the dataset is a fixed input to the model.
Data Centric AI, on the other hand, focuses on improving the model by improving the dataset. The idea is to systematically or algorithmically improve the dataset, as opposed to having humans change the dataset. This approach is built on the principle that the quality of the dataset is directly correlated to the quality of the predictions. Therefore, by improving the dataset, you can improve the overall performance of the model.
Our goal is to start thinking about Machine Learning in terms of data and not the model. This shift in perspective can help us better understand the importance of the dataset in the AI process.
To illustrate this point, let's take the K nearest neighbors algorithm as an example. K nearest neighbors is not a Data Centric AI algorithm, but it will help us understand how we can think about AI in terms of data.
K nearest neighbors involves finding the K nearest neighbors to a new point and classifying that point based on majority voting. The key idea here is that there is no loss function. All decisions are based on data, and the quality of the data is as related as possible to the quality of the prediction. Therefore, fixing the data will improve your model.
By the end of this lecture, you will know what a Data Centric AI algorithm is and how it is different from K nearest neighbors. You will have a better understanding of the importance of data in the AI process and how improving the dataset can improve the overall performance of the model.
4. What is data-centric AI
Artificial intelligence is a rapidly evolving field with two main approaches to building models: model-centric and data-centric. Model-centric AI prioritizes the development of the most sophisticated and complex models possible, while data-centric AI prioritizes the quality and quantity of the data used to train the model.
Data-centric AI approaches are gaining more attention recently, as they can provide more accurate results and identify issues with the data used to train the model. On the other hand, model-centric AI approaches can also be useful in specific scenarios, such as when there is a lack of high-quality data for training.
When it comes to building models, it's crucial to understand the difference between these two approaches. Data-centric AI tasks include:
outlier detection and removal,
error detection and correction,
data augmentation,
feature engineering and selection,
establishing consensus labels,
active learning,
and curriculum learning,
all of which help to improve the quality of the data used to train the model.
For instance, outlier detection and removal is important because it helps to ensure that the model is not overly influenced by a few incorrect data points. Error detection and correction is crucial because it helps to ensure that the data is correctly labeled and minimize label errors. Data augmentation is a technique that can increase the size of a dataset by generating additional data points, which can help to improve the accuracy of the model. Feature engineering and selection involves selecting the most relevant features from a dataset and using them to train the model.
Establishing consensus labels is important when multiple people are labeling data points, as it ensures that there is a high level of agreement among the different annotators. Active learning is a technique that can be used to optimize the model's performance by selecting the most informative data points for labeling, while curriculum learning involves gradually increasing the complexity of the training data to help the model learn more effectively.
5. Why is data-centric AI becoming so important?
Overall, data-centric AI is gaining more attention because it can help to ensure that the model is accurate, reliable, and effective. Bad data can be very problematic and cost businesses, governments, and economies billions of dollars. Data-centric AI techniques can help to solve this issue by prioritizing the quality and quantity of the data used to train the model.
It's worth noting that data quality is essential for real-world technologies, such as DALL-E and ChatGPT. For example, DALL-E generates images, but it can be limited by incorrectly labeled objects. Similarly, ChatGPT had to deal with biased and inaccurate outputs by using people to rank the quality of the data and update the model accordingly.
Thus, data-centric AI is a critical field that can have significant impacts on various industries. By prioritizing the quality and quantity of the data used to train the model, we can ensure that our models are accurate, reliable, and effective. By understanding the various data-centric AI tasks and their importance, we can continue to improve our models and make the most of this exciting field.
6. Tesla's data engine
If you're interested in machine learning, you're undoubtedly familiar with Tesla. At a talk by Andrej Karpathy, formerly Tesla's Director of AI, Tesla's data engine was showcased.
Tesla trains its self-driving Model by first identifying problems, such as the car acting strangely in tunnels due to a lack of tunnel data.
Tesla then collects more data in tunnels, updates their training data, labels it, redeploy it, and sees what breaks.
This is a challenging, iterative process that requires sending the car out to see where it fails and then collecting more data. Automating the process of identifying missing data and outliers could have reduced these cycles and gotten the car out faster.
Another example is navigating traffic lights. Elon Musk challenged Andrej to get the car to navigate any street in the world, including stopping at traffic lights. This may seem like a simple problem, but it's actually very complicated and messy. Finding systematic ways to group data and train in a robust and reliable way is crucial. Real-world data is often highly complex, so Andre's key takeaway is that we need to focus on improving data-centric approaches.
A common use case is training a model with noisy labels, where cats are labeled dogs and vice versa. The classical approach is to rely on human-powered solutions, such as paying for more high-quality data or building custom tools. But the goal is to systematize these approaches to make them more reliable and accurate.
It's important to note that improving data-centric approaches is crucial because real-world data is highly complex and messy. Machine learning models heavily rely on data, so improving data quality and approaches is crucial in improving the performance of machine learning models.
In summary, data-centric approaches are highly effective in improving the performance of machine learning models. These approaches modify the dataset to improve the quality of the data, making it more reliable and accurate. This is crucial, as machine learning models heavily rely on data.
