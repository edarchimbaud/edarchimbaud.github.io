Renaissance Technologies
What is Renaissance's Medallion strategy? If you've heard of Renaissance Technologies, the secretive Long Island quantitative hedge fund founded by mathematician Jim Simons, you're probably aware of its outstanding performance. But what you may not have realized is just how extraordinary that performance was.
What are the historical returns of the Medallion Fund?
As Greg Zuckerman noted in The Man Who Solved the Market, Renaissance's flagship Medallion fund generated annualized returns of 66% (before fees) and 39% (net of fees) from 1988 to 2018.
To put this performance in perspective, $1 invested in the Medallion Fund from 1988 to 2018 would have grown to over $20,000 (net of fees). 1 invested in the S&P 500 would have grown to only $20 over the same period. Even a $1 investment in Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway would have only returned $100 during that time.
That means the Medallion fund outperformed one of the best asset classes of the past few decades by 1,000 times. And that it outperformed one of the best investors of all time by 200 times!
How does Medallion correlate with the market?
As impressive as their respective performances have been, comparing the Medallion fund's annual returns (net of fees) to those of the S&P 500 takes their achievement to another level:
Not only has the Medallion Fund lost money in only one year since its inception, net of fees (1989), but more importantly, the fund's returns have been partially negatively correlated with the market (correlation = -0.41). For example, in 2008, the S&P 500 lost 37%. The Medallion fund, on the other hand, had a gain of 82% net of fees!
What is the strategy of the Renaissance Medallion Fund?
That's the multi-billion dollar question. One possibility is that Medallion is simply a better market maker in the world and that, over millions of trades, this advantage translates into observed returns.
In fact, the broad outlines of Medallion's core strategy are publicly known. It's the details that are secret. And there are millions of details, and they are critical to performance. However, the question was about the strategy, so that's what I'll try to answer.
Statistical arbitrage
The basic strategy is statistical arbitrage at the portfolio level, pushed to the limit and extremely well executed. Basically, portfolios of long (buy) and short (sell) positions are created to hedge market risk, sector risk and any other type of risk that Renaissance can statistically predict.
The extreme degree of hedging reduces this net rate of return. But portfolio volatility is reduced by an even more important factor. The standard deviation of the portfolio's value at a future date is much lower than its expected value. Therefore, with a large number of trades, the law of large numbers ensures that the probability of a loss is very low. In this situation, leverage multiplies the expected return and volatility by the same multiple. Thus, even with high leverage, the probability of a loss remains very low.
Multifactorial pricing
The general properties of the strategy can be inferred from Renaissance's statement for the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations hearing, dated July 22, 2014.
It notes that Renaissance collects "all publicly available data that they believe can influence the price movements of tradable instruments: news articles, analyst reports, energy reports, crop reports, weather reports, regulatory agency findings, accounting data, and, of course, quotes and transactions in markets around the world."
It also notes that their models "use this data to make predictions about future price changes."
High frequency
The hearing was specifically about the Medallion fund. The statement said, "The model developed by Renaissance for Medallion makes predictions that are only marginally profitable."
According to Robert Mercer, one of the lead managers, Medallion was only right on about 50.75 percent of its trades. Nonetheless, of the millions of transactions, that percentage helped the company make billions.
This approach is not unique in the market. The market maker Timber Hill (founded by Thomas Peterffy) and now acquired by Two Sigma used the same approach. On the other hand, the execution of the Medallion strategy is particularly careful. Medallion excels at predicting the cost of a transaction.
Hedging
Renaissance does not hesitate to implement a very sophisticated hedging system. Through years of use and refinement, Renaissance knows that its models are very reliable. There is always a risk that something will happen that is not covered by the models. In particular, there is always the risk of something that is outside of previous experience. This is called a "black swan" event (see the excellent book by Nassim Taleb on the subject).
To further reduce its risk, Renaissance takes a call option on the portfolio it has designed. This call option idea is therefore excellent. It allows for high leverage and offers protection against both the very low probability of a loss greater than the option premium and the unknown probability of a potentially catastrophic loss due to a black swan event.
